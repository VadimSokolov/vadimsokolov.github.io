Review of Basic Probability Concepts¶

Probability lets us talk efficiently about things that we are uncertain about.

  • What will Amazon’s sales be next quarter?

  • What will the return be on my stocks next year?

  • How often will users click on a particular Google ad?

All these involve estimating or predicting unknowns!!

Random Variables¶

  • Random Variables are numbers that we are not sure about.
  • There's a list of potential outcomes.
  • We assign probabilities to each outcome.

Example: Suppose that we are about to toss two coins.

  • Let $X$ denote the number of heads.
  • We call $X$ the random variable that stands for the potential outcome.

Probability¶

Probability is a language designed to help us communicate about uncertainty.

We assign a number between $0$ and $1$ measuring how likely that event is to occur

It's immensely useful, and there's only a few basic rules.

  1. If an event $A$ is certain to occur, it has probability $1$, denoted $P(A)##1$

  2. Either an event $A$ occurs or it does not. $$\begin{aligned} P(\mbox{not A}) ## 1 - P(\mbox{A}) \end{aligned}$$

  3. If two events are mutually exclusive (both cannot occur simultaneously) then $P( \mbox{A or B} ) ## P(A) +P(B)$

  4. Conditional probability $P( \mbox{A and B} ) ## P( A| B) P(B)$

$Pr ( {\rm Rose \; Hits \; Safely \; in \; 44 \; consecutive \; games} ) ##$

$P ( A_1 \; \text{and} \; A_2 \ldots \text{and} \; A_{44} ) ## P ( A_1 ) P ( A_2 ) \ldots P ( A_{44} )$

We now need to find $P(A_i) \ldots$ where $\mathbb{P} (A_i ) ## 1 - \mathbb{P} ( \text{not} \; A_i )$

So for the winning streak we have $(0.76)^{44} ## 0.0000057$ !!!

Conditional, Joint and Marginal Distributions¶

We want to use probability to describe outocmes involving more than one variable at a time. We need to be able to measure what we think will happen to one variable relative to another

In general the notation is ...

  • $pr ( X##x, Y##y )$ is joint probability the that $Y$ equals $y$ and $X##x$

  • $pr ( X##x | Y##y )$ is the conditional probability that $Y$ equals $y$ given $X##x$

  • $pr ( X##x )$ is the marginal probability of $X##x$

Conditional, Joint and Marginal Distributions¶

Relationship between the joint and conditional ... $$ \begin{aligned} pr(x,y) & ## p(x) p(y|x) \\ & ## p(y) p(x|y) \end{aligned} $$

Relationship between the joint and marginal ... $$ \begin{aligned} pr(x) & ## \sum_y p(x,y) \\ pr(y) & ## \sum_x p(x,y) \end{aligned} $$

Independence¶

Two random variable $X$ and $Y$ are independent if

$$Pr (Y ## y |X ## x) ## pr (Y ## y)$$ for all possible $x$ and $y$ values.

Knowing $X##x$ tells you nothing about $Y$!

Example: Tossing a coin twice. What's the probability of getting $H$ in the second toss given we saw a $T$ in the first one?

Bayes Rule¶

Key fact: $Pr(x|y)$ is generally different from $Pr (y|x)$!

Example: Most people would agree $$\begin{aligned} Pr & \left ( Practice \; hard | Play \; in \; NBA \right ) \approx 1\\ Pr & \left ( Play \; in \; NBA | Practice \; hard \right ) \approx 0\end{aligned}$$

The main reason for the difference is that $Pr ( Play \; in \; NBA ) \approx 0$.

Bayes Rule¶

The computation of $Pr (x|y)$ from $Pr (x)$ and $Pr (y|x)$ is called Bayes theorem ...

$$pr(x|y) ## \frac{p(y,x)}{pr(y)} ## \frac{p(y,x)}{\sum_x pr(y,x)} ## \frac{pr(y|x)pr(x)}{\sum_x pr(y|x)pr(x)}$$

This shows now the conditional distribution is related to the joint and marginal distributions. You'll be given all the quantities on the r.h.s.

Random Variables: Expectation $E(X)$¶

  • The expected value of a random variable is simply a weighted average of the possible values X can assume.

  • The weights are the probabilities of occurrence of those values.

    $$E(X) ## \sum_x xP(X##x)$$

    We can also calculate $E(X^2) ## \sum_x x^2 P(X##x)$

Standard Deviation $sd (X)$ and Variance $Var(X)$¶

The variance is calculated as $$Var(X) ## E \left ( (X - E(X))^2 \right )$$ A simpler calculation is $Var(X) ## E ( X^2) - E(X)^2$.

The standard deviation is the square-root of variance. $$sd(X) ## \sqrt{Var(X)}$$

Covariance¶

Suppose that we have two random variables $X$ and $Y$

We need to measure whether they move together or in opposite directions

The Covariance is defined by

$$Cov(X,Y) ## E \left ( ( X- E(X) )(Y- E(Y)) \right )$$

The Correlation is defined by

$$Cov(X,Y) ## \sum_{x,y} ( x - E(X) )(y - E(Y))pr (x,y)$$

Correlation¶

The is defined by $$Corr(X,Y) ## \frac{ Cov(X,Y) }{ sd(X) sd(Y) }$$

  • What are the units of $Corr(X,Y)$?
    They don't depend on the units of $X$ or $Y$!
  • $ - 1 \leq Corr(X,Y) \leq 1 $

If $Cov ( Apple \; Sales, Economy ) ## 5$, $sd( Apple \; Sales ) ## 2$ and $sd ( Economy ) ## 3.5$, then there’s a $71.4$% correlation $$Corr ( Apple \; Sales, Economy ) ## \frac{5}{2 \times 3.5} ## \frac{5}{7} ## 0.714$$

Binomial Distribution¶

Bernoulli Trials:

A sequence of repeated experiments are Bernoulli trials if:

  1. The result of each trial is either a success or failure.

  2. The probability $p$ of a success is the same for all trials.

  3. The trials are independent.

If $X$ is the number of successes it is a Binomial Random Variable.

Binomial Distribution¶

We calculate probabilities using: $$\mbox{P}(X##x) ## {n \choose x} p^x(1-p)^{n-x}$$

$n \choose x$ counts the number of ways of getting $x$successes in $n$ trials.

  • The formula for $n \choose x$ is $${n \choose x} ## \frac{n!}{x!(n-x)!}$$ where $n!##n\times(n-1)\times(n-2)\times...\times 2 \times 1$.

In R: pbinom(z, n, p) and rbinom(1000, n, p)

Binomial Distribution¶

The Mean and Variance of the Binomial are:

Binomial Distribution Parameters
Expected value $$\mu ## E(X) ## n p$$
Variance $$\sigma^2 ## Var (X) ## n p ( 1 - p )$$

  • Used for discrete probability modeling

  • Finds properties of proportions

Bernoulli Process¶

Let $X$ represent either success ($X##1$) or failure ($X##0$)

  • A Bernoulli process is a binary outcome with $$p( X##1 ) ##p \; \; {\rm and} \; \; p( X##0 ) ## 1-p$$

  • We assume that trials are independent.

The probability of two successes in a row is $$ \begin{aligned} p( X_1 ## 1 , X_2 ## 1 ) ##& p( X_1 ## 1) p( X_2 ## 1) \\ ##& p \cdot p \\ ##& p^2 \end{aligned} $$

Binomial distribution counts the successes of a Bernoulli process.

Normal Distribution with General Mean and Variance¶

Here are two useful facts: If $X \sim \mbox{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $$\begin{aligned} P(\mu - 2.58 \sigma < X < \mu + 2.58 \sigma) &##& 0.99 \\ P(\mu - 1.96 \sigma < X < \mu + 1.96 \sigma) &##& 0.95 \, .\end{aligned}$$

  • The chance that $X$ will be within $2.58 \sigma$ of its mean is $99\%$, and the chance that it will be within $2\sigma$ of its mean is about $95\%$.

The Normal Distribution¶

Our probability model is written $X \sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$

$\mu$ is the mean, $\sigma^2$ is the variance

  • Standardization if $X \sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ then $$Z ##\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0,1)$$

  • $\mu:$ the center of the distribution

    $\sigma :$ how spread out the data are

    $95$% probability $X \in \mu \pm 1.96 \sigma$.

Step 2: List the known conditional probabilities

Test Marketing¶

Two Headed Coin¶

Let $E$ be the event that you get $10$ Heads in a row $$P \left ( {\rm two \; headed} | E \right ) ## \frac{ P \left ( E | {\rm two \; headed} \right )P \left ( {\rm two \; headed} \right )} {P \left ( E | {\rm fair} \right )P \left ( {\rm fair} \right ) + P \left ( E | {\rm two \; headed} \right )P \left ( {\rm two \; headed} \right )}$$ Therefore, the posterior probability $$P \left ( {\rm two \; headed} | E \right ) ## \frac{ 1 \times \frac{1}{1025} }{ \frac{1}{1024} \times \frac{1024}{1025} + 1 \times \frac{1}{1025} } ## 0.50$$

Next Toss?¶

What's the probability that the next toss is a head?

  • Using the law of total probability gives $$\begin{aligned} P( H |E ) &## P( H | {\rm two \; headed} )P( {\rm two \; headed} |E ) + P( H | {\rm fair} )P( {\rm fair} |E) \\ & ## 1 \times \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} \\ & ## \frac{3}{4}\end{aligned}$$

What if they decide not to play $50/50$?

  • Then there’s a mixed strategy (randomized) that is a Nash equilibrium that is, both players won’t deviate from the strategy.

  • We’ll see the the following equilibrium happens

    1. $A$ plays Red with probability $(1/2)$ and blue $(1/2)$

    2. $B$ plays Red with probability $(1/4)$ and blue $(3/4)$

Probability Matrix¶

Nash equilibrium probabilities

  • We have: $p##P( A \; red )## 1/2, p_1 ## P( B \; red ) ## 1/4$
$(A,B)$ Probability
$P( red, red )$ (1/2)(1/4)##1/8
$P( red, blue )$ (1/2)(3/4)##3/8
$P( blue, red )$ (1/2)(1/4)##1/8
$P( blue, blue )$ (1/2)(3/4)##3/8

Expected payoff¶

Expected payoff to playing Red equals that of playing Blue for both.

  • $A$'s expected payoff is $\frac{3}{4}$ and $B$'s is \$1

    $$\begin{aligned} E(A) & ## (1/8)(3) + (3/8)(1) ## 3/4 \\ E(B) & ##1 \end{aligned}$$

  • $B$ is the favored position.

Optimal Strategy¶

We have general payoffs: $p##P( A \; red ), p_1 ## P( B \; red )$ $$\begin{aligned} f_A ( p , p_1 ) &##& 3 p p_1 + ( 1 -p ) ( 1 - p_1 ) \\ f_B ( p , p_1 ) &##& 2 \{ p(1 - p_1) + ( 1 -p ) p_1 \}\end{aligned}$$ To find the equilibrium point $$\begin{aligned} ( \partial / \partial p ) f_A ( p , p_1 ) &##& 3 p_1 - ( 1 - p_1 ) ## 4 p_1 -1 \; \; {\rm so} \; \; p_1## 1/4 \\ ( \partial / \partial p_1 ) f_B ( p , p_1 ) &##& 2 ( 1 - 2p ) \; \; {\rm so} \; \; p## 1/2\end{aligned}$$

Catastrophe Modeling¶

You live in a house that is somewhat prone to mud slides.

  • Each rainy season there is a 1\% chance of a mud slide occurring. - You estimate that a mud slide would do \$1 million in damage.

  • You have the option of building a retaining wall that would help reduce the chance of a devastating mud slide.

The wall costs \$40,000 to build, and if the slide occurs, the wall will hold with a 95\% probability.

  • You also have the option of a Geologist's opinion.

  Should you build the wall?

Should you use the Geologist's Test and Bayes Rule?

Decision Tree¶

Let's formally solve this as follows:

  • Build a decision tree.

  • The tree will list the probabilities at each node. It will also list any costs there are you going down a particular branch.

  • Finally, it will list the expected cost of going down each branch, so we can see which one has the better risk/reward characteristics.

There's also the possibility of a further test to see if the wall will hold.

Testing¶

Let's include the testing option

  • You also have the option of having a test done to determine whether or not a slide will occur in your location.

  • The test costs \$3000 and has the following accuracies.

    $$P( T | {\rm Slide} ) ## 0.90 \; \; {\rm and } \; \; P( \bar{T} | {\rm No \; Slide} ) ## 0.85$$

If you choose the test, then should you build the wall?

Bayes Rule¶

Bayes Rule is as follows:

  • The initial prior probabilities are $$P( Slide ) ## 0.01 \; \; {\rm and} \; \; P ( {\rm No \; Slide} ) ## 0.99$$

  • Therefore $$\begin{aligned} P( T) & ## P( T | {\rm Slide} ) P( {\rm Slide} ) + P( T | {\rm No \; Slide} ) P( {\rm No \; Slide} ) \\ P(T)& ## 0.90 \times 0.01 + 0.15 \times 0.99 ## 0.1575\end{aligned}$$ We'll use this to find our optimal course of action.

Bayes Probabilities¶

$P(Slide|T)$¶

The Bayes probability given a positive test is $$\begin{aligned} P ( Slide | T ) & ## \frac{ P ( T | Slide ) P ( Slide )}{P(T)} \\ & ## \frac{ 0.90 \times 0.01}{ 0.1575} ## 0.0571\end{aligned}$$

Bayes Probabilities¶

$P(Slide| Not \; T )$¶

The Bayes probability given a negative test is $$\begin{aligned} P \left ( {\rm Slide} | \bar{T} \right ) & ## \frac{ P ( \bar{T} | {\rm Slide} ) P ( {\rm Slide} )}{P(\bar{T})} \\ & ## \frac{0.1 \times 0.01 }{0.8425} \\ & ##0.001187\end{aligned}$$

Compare this to the initial base rate of a $1$% chance of having a mud slide.

Probability Lose Everything¶

You build the wall without testing, what's the probability that you lose everything?

With the given situation, there is one path (or sequence of events and decisions) that leads to losing everything:

  1. Build without testing (given)

  2. Slide ($0.01$)

  3. Doesn't hold ($0.05$)

$$P ( {\rm losing} \; {\rm everything} | {\rm build} \; {\rm w/o} \; {\rm testing} ) ## 0.01 \times 0.05 ## 0.0005$$

Probability Lose Everything¶

You choose the test, what's the probability that you'll lose everything?

There are two paths that lead to losing everything:

  • First Path: There are three things that have to happen to lose everything

    Test +ve ($P##0.1575$), Build, Slide ($P## 0.0571$), Doesn't Hold ($P##0.05$)

  • Second Path: Now you lose everything if

    Test -ve ($P##0.8425$), Don't Build, Slide given negative ($P##0.001187$)

Conditional Probabilities¶

  • For the first path $$P ( {\rm first} \; {\rm path} ) ## 0.1575 \times 0.0571 \times 0.05 ## 0.00045$$

  • For the path $$P ( {\rm second} \; {\rm path} ) ## 0.8425 \times 0.001187 ## 0.00101$$

Hence putting it all together $$P ( {\rm losing} \; {\rm everything} | {\rm testing} ) ## 0.00045 + 0.00101 ## {\color{red}{0.00146}}$$
